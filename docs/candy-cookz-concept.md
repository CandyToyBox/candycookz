# Candy Cookz — Concept Brief

**Domain:** candycookz.com
**One-liner:** Productized creative "cooks" for Web3 and AI brands. You order a cook, drop your ingredients, I fire up the grill, you eat. Want more? Smash the cook button.

---

## Why this exists

Negotiating rates is the worst part of freelancing, and it's where you lose money. Candy Cookz removes negotiation entirely:

- **Fixed prices.** No quotes, no haggling. The menu is the menu.
- **Paid upfront.** Money lands before you cook. No chasing invoices.
- **You control the output.** A "cook" buys you firing up the grill on a topic — *you* decide how much comes off it. Could be 3 pieces, could be 12. That protects you from underpricing your own effort, because effort isn't what's being sold. The *cook* is.
- **Async by default.** Clients self-serve from the menu, submit materials, and wait for the plate. One-on-one chat only for bigger cooks.
- **Scarcity built in.** Only a few seats at the grill per cook type. When they're full, that cook is "sold out" until you clear the line.

This is your answer to "I'm bad at talking about rates." You never talk about rates. The grill does.

## The brand

**Aesthetic:** Open-flame grill / hibachi / teppanyaki (Benihana-style cook-in-front-of-you) with a Texas BBQ edge. Think glossy, juicy, colorful skewers and kebabs — vibrant peppers, flame, char marks, steam. Appetizing and warm, **but it is not about food.** Food is the metaphor. The product is *cooking the content.*

**Vibe words:** Fresh, hot, hand-made, a little experimental, fun, satisfying. The opposite of a sterile agency.

**Palette direction:** Flame and char — deep charcoal/black base, with hot oranges, reds, flame-yellow, and a few juicy accent colors (lime green, cherry red, glaze-purple) like grilled veg on a skewer. Glossy highlights so things look *delicious.*

**Tone of voice:** Playful, confident, food-pun-forward but not corny. "Order a cook." "Drop your ingredients." "Fresh off the grill." "Smash the cook button." "Tonight's specials." "Seats at the grill."

**Audience — strict:** Web3 and AI-native brands only. New tokens, protocols, hackathons, mints, AI startups. **Not** traditional Web2 / classic graphic-design clients — they won't get the model or the speed, and they'll want pixel-level art direction that isn't what you offer.

You're an **AI-and-Canva-native creator**, not a classically trained Adobe vector/After-Effects designer — and for this audience that's a *strength*, not a caveat. Web3/AI brands move fast and want volume, iteration, and someone fluent in the same tools they use.

What you do great:

- Sponsorship decks, event branding, web pages

You don't need to claim to be something you're not. The menu sells outcomes and *taste*, not technique.

## The "Cook" mechanic (core loop)

1. **Order** — Client picks a cook from the menu and pays upfront.
2. **Drop ingredients** — They submit materials: logo, brand kit, content brief, the idea. (Required fields depend on the cook.)
3. **Cook** — You go off feel. You make what you think is right — a few pieces, several versions, however the grill runs hot that day.
4. **Plate it** — You deliver when you've cooked as much as feels right. Light edits included.
5. **Smash the Cook Button** — If they love it and want more, they pay again and you keep cooking. Repeat orders → a standing reservation (retainer).

The "smash the cook button" upsell is where the income actually grows. Entry prices stay low and friction-free; the money compounds through repeat cooks and retainers.

### Serving levels (confirmed)
Let bigger appetites pay more without you negotiating:
- **Single Serving** = the base cook
- **Family Style** = bigger batch, more variations (~2–3x price)
- **Keep Cooking** = the repeat-order button (retainer on-ramp) — see pricing logic below

### Reserved Seating (retainer)
After a client smashes the Keep Cooking button a couple times, offer **Reserved Seating** — a standing monthly seat with agreed deliverables, $1,500–$4,000/mo depending on the cooks included. This is the real income engine — same logic as the Contra rate card.

## Pricing philosophy (confirmed)

- **Prices rise with experience — but only on the next batch.** When you "restock" a cook, new buyers pay the new (higher) price. You never raise prices on existing clients mid-relationship. Stability for them, growth for you.
- **The Keep Cooking button gets repriced after the first batch.** Once you've done the first cook for a brand and know what you're working with, you set their continue price deliberately:
  - **Love the brand + low difficulty →** price Keep Cooking *low* to keep them ordering and steer toward Reserved Seating.
  - **Don't want more of it →** price Keep Cooking *high* so declining is automatic and graceful. The menu says no for you. No awkward conversation.
- This turns your pricing into a quiet filter: good-fit clients get cheaper to continue, bad-fit clients price themselves out — and you never negotiate either way.

## Payments & agentic commerce

Checkout supports **both** traditional and crypto rails, plus machine-to-machine ordering:

- **Stripe** — cards, for clients who want the familiar path.
- **Crypto** — wallet payment (stablecoin), on-brand for the Web3 audience.
- **x402 (agentic payments)** — the standout. Expose cooks over the x402 protocol so **AI agents can order, pay per request in stablecoin, and receive the delivered content autonomously** — no human in the loop. An agent browses the menu, fires a cook, settles payment over HTTP, gets the result back.

Why this matters: as agents start buying services on behalf of brands, Candy Cookz can be one of the first creative shops they can actually transact with. It fits the "productized, fixed-price, async, paid-upfront" model perfectly — agents need exactly that: clear price, clear deliverable, instant settlement. Worth building Stripe + crypto first for launch, then layering x402 as the headline differentiator.

> Practical note: x402 cooks should be your *deterministic* ones, or ones where the whole point is a **human's eyes**. An agent knows exactly what it's buying. The looser "I cook by feel" cooks are better suited to human clients who enjoy the surprise.

### Agent Menu — cooks available over x402 first

These are the cooks an AI agent can order, pay for, and receive autonomously. Two flavors: deterministic creative output, and "human-in-the-loop" insight an agent literally cannot produce itself.

**Human-in-the-loop (your edge — an agent can't get this anywhere else):**
- **Human Feedback Cook** — An agent builds an app and needs a real human to test it and give honest, insider feedback. You walk through it and deliver written notes + a short video with your face on camera (proof a real human did it). Same for customer-style feedback.
- **Web3 Brain Cook (AMA / voice / opinion)** — An agent needs a genuine human Web3 perspective: a brainstorm, a gut-check, a take, an "ask me anything." You give real human voice and opinion the agent can't synthesize.

**Deterministic creative:**
- **Logo Cook** — ~3 logo variations from their name/brief.
- **Theme / Style Cook** — ~3 brand direction concepts from a name + the direction they want.
- **Image Cook** — a batch of AI + Canva images, set number of variations.
- **Social Graphics Cook** — flyers, announcement banners, social posts, YouTube thumbnails — fixed deliverable counts.

Each agent cook needs a crisp, machine-readable promise ("you get exactly X") so an agent can decide to buy with no human in the loop.

## Scarcity & gamification

- **Limited seats per cook.** Maybe only 1–2 Sponsorship Deck cooks open at once. When full, show **"Sold Out — back when the grill clears."** Creates urgency and protects you from overcommitting.
- **Tonight's specials.** Rotate featured cooks to keep the menu fresh and signal what you're in the mood to make.
- **Portfolio = the menu's "dishes we've served."** Each brand you've worked with gets its own section (like your Contra profile), so buyers browse real plates before ordering.
- **Smash-the-cook-button** is itself the game loop: order → love it → smash → more. Satisfying, low-stakes, repeatable.

## What to build first (suggested order)

1. **Lock the brand** — name's done. Next: logo, palette, the grill/kebab visual system, voice.
2. **Write the menu** — finalize cook names, what's guaranteed in each, prices, seat counts.
3. **Build the site** — homepage (the menu) + per-cook order pages + portfolio sections. Stripe/crypto checkout that collects payment + an intake form (their ingredients/brief) in one step.
4. **Set the intake** — a simple form per cook: brand assets, brand kit, brief, links. So cooking starts the moment they pay.
5. **Soft launch** — open a few cheap cooks (Image, Content, Album Art) to get the loop working and collect testimonials, then add premium cooks.

## Edit / re-cook policy (confirmed)

Every cook includes **3–4 small tweaks** — wording, colors, swapping an element, trying a different version. Nothing that restructures the whole thing.

Plus **one major redo** is allowed *if* the result was a real misunderstanding or completely the wrong direction (and only when you feel it's warranted). On a full redo, you remake it and throw in a couple of tweaks on the new version. After that one big structural edit, further structural changes become a new cook.

Stated simply for the site:

> Each cook includes up to 3–4 minor tweaks, plus one full redo if the direction was genuinely off. Bigger changes after that = a fresh cook.
